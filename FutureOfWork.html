Summary Deep Dive

The Future of Work with AI Agents

What do we *actually* want AI to do for us?

AI is coming for our jobs... or is it?

We hear a lot about AI taking over tasks. But has anyone stopped to ask the people actually *doing* those jobs what they want? This research from Stanford does exactly that, creating a groundbreaking framework to audit the future of work.

Introducing WORKBank & The Human Agency Scale

The researchers built WORKBank, a massive database of 844 tasks across 104 occupations. They surveyed 1,500 workers and 52 AI experts to understand two key things: what workers desire from AI, and what AI is actually capable of today.

It's Not Just "Automate or Not"

The study introduces the Human Agency Scale (HAS) to move beyond a simple automate/don't automate binary. It measures the ideal level of human involvement.

H1

AI handles it all. No human needed.

H2

AI needs minimal human input.

H3

Equal partnership. Humans and AI collaborate.

H4

Human drives the task, with AI assisting.

H5

Human is essential. AI can't function without them.

Finding: Most workers (45.2% of occupations) prefer an H3 Equal Partnership, showing a strong desire for collaboration, not just replacement.

The Desire vs. Capability Landscape

By plotting what workers want against what AI can do, the study identifies four critical zones for the future of work.

Automation "Green Light" Zone

High desire, high capability. These are prime tasks for AI automation that workers would welcome. (e.g., scheduling appointments, checking data for quality control).

Automation "Red Light" Zone

High capability, low desire. AI can do these tasks, but workers don't want it to. Deploying AI here could cause friction. (e.g., writing creative stories, contacting vendors).

R&D Opportunity Zone

High desire, low capability. Workers want help here, but the tech isn't ready yet. A huge opportunity for AI research. (e.g., preparing budgets, designing game prototypes).

Low Priority Zone

Low desire, low capability. Neither workers nor the technology are ready for these tasks. (e.g., observing pictures, tracing lost baggage).

What Does This All Mean?

There's a Mismatch in Investment

A shocking 41% of Y Combinator company-task mappings are in the "Red Light" or "Low Priority" zones. We're building AI for things people don't want automated, while ignoring things they do.

The Future is Interpersonal

As AI handles more data analysis, the most valuable human skills are shifting. Organizing, planning, training, and communicating are becoming the new high-value competencies where human agency is most desired.

Workers Want Partnership, Not Replacement

The dominant desire is for AI to be a collaborator (H3), freeing up humans to focus on high-value work, not replacing them entirely. This is a crucial insight for responsible AI development.

Based on the paper "Future of Work with AI Agents" by Shao, Y., et al., Stanford University.

Future of Work: A Technical Deep Dive

The Auditing Framework & WORKBank

To understand the potential impact of AI agents, the study created a novel auditing framework. This framework moves beyond simple automation metrics to capture nuanced worker desires and align them with current technological capabilities.

Task-Level Auditing

The audit focuses on complex, multi-step tasks from the U.S. Dept. of Labor's O*NET database, reflecting real-world job responsibilities rather than isolated actions.

WORKBank Database

The framework was used to build WORKBank, a database covering 844 tasks across 104 occupations, with input from 1,500 domain workers and 52 AI experts.

The Human Agency Scale (HAS)

A key innovation is the Human Agency Scale (HAS), a 5-level scale that quantifies the desired level of human involvement, providing a shared language for discussing automation vs. augmentation.

The study found that an "Equal Partnership" (H3) is the most desired level of collaboration for 45.2% of occupations, indicating a strong preference for augmentation over full automation.

The Desire-Capability Landscape

By plotting worker desire against expert-rated AI capability, the framework identifies four distinct zones that can guide R&D and investment.

Green Light Zone

High desire, high capability. Prime targets for deployment.

Red Light Zone

High capability, low desire. Deploy with caution.

R&D Opportunity Zone

High desire, low capability. Key areas for future research.

Low Priority Zone

Low desire, low capability. Not a current focus.

Key Findings & Implications

The analysis reveals critical insights for the future of work:

  • Investment Mismatch

    Current AI startup investment (proxied by YC companies) is misaligned with worker desires. 41% of company-task mappings fall into the "Red Light" or "Low Priority" zones, indicating a disconnect between what is being built and what is wanted.

  • Shift in Core Skills

    As AI takes on more analytical tasks, the value of human skills is shifting. Interpersonal skills (like training, communicating, organizing) are becoming more critical and are associated with higher levels of desired human agency, even if they aren't the highest-paid skills today.

Technical summary based on "Future of Work with AI Agents" by Shao, Y., et al., Stanford University.
